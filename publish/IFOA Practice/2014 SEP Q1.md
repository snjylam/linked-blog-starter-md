<u><b>Q1(i): What are asset shares used for in a with-profits fund?</b></u>
**Treating customers fairly**
- Policy asset shares are widely used as a benchmark to determine the level of payouts to with-profits policyholders as an input to treating customers fairly.
- Asset shares may be used as a guide to determine a fair payout, however, a payout different to the asset share is not necessarily unfair.
- The definition and the way companies use asset shares are used must be consistent with the PPFM.

**Maturity values**
- Most companies using the “addition to benefits” method use asset shares as a guide to determine maturity values.
- Terminal bonus can be calculated using asset shares for the maturing cohort;
- by comparing the asset share to the contractual benefits of the policy;
- such that the terminal bonus is the excess of the asset share over the contractual benefits subject to the smoothing policy.
- The smoothing profit/loss is calculated as the excess of the asset share over the actual payments.
- Firms may specify target ranges around 100% of the asset share within which 90% of maturity payouts must lie.
- Monitoring smoothing profit/loss each year can help companies ensure it pays out an average asset share over time.
- This ensures smoothed payouts are fair overall.

**Surrender values**
- Many companies use asset shares in setting surrender values;
- including market value reductions on unitised with-profits business.
- Typically, less smoothing is applied to surrenders than to maturities.
- The surrender value may deviate from the asset share as the policy reaches maturity;
- in order to ensure the surrender value runs smoothly into maturity value.
- At early durations, the surrender value may be higher than the asset share;
- e.g. due to PRE, policyholders may expect a surrender approximately equal to return of premiums.
- Asset shares may also be used for altered benefits;
- e.g. for paid-up policies.

**Regular bonus**
- Asset shares can be used as a guide to set the level of regular bonus.
- The bonus earning capacity indicates the sustainable level of regular bonus
- The bonus capacity can be calculated by equating asset share to a realistic gross premium valuation;
- allowing some target terminal bonus;
- with the regular bonus as the variable.
- Alternatively, a stochastic model can be used to project asset shares and bonus overtime.
- This allows for the company’s policy on the relationship between regular and terminal bonus;
- so, the company can set a regular bonus strategy that would be supportable in all but the most extreme scenarios.
- Asset shares can also be used to compare the bonus earning capacity of new business and in-force business in order to determine the need for a new bonus series and to set premium rates.

**Policy projections**
- Various documents may need to be issued at point of sale or during the policy lifetime that require maturity and surrender values based on asset shares.
- The starting point for these projections would be the current asset share;
- which would be projected using the described asset share methodology.

**Regulatory reporting**
- Asset shares are also used for establishing realistic solvency positions, either on economic or regulatory basis.
- Since asset shares are used to determine payouts, they are used to calculate BEL under Solvency II;
- including the cost of guarantees;
- and similarly in solvency capital requirement assessments.

**Inherited estate**
- Asset shares may be used to determine how inherited estate is distributed, if the company decides to make such an allocation.

<u><b>Q1(ii): What are the uses of inherited estate within a with-profits fund?</b></u>
**Capital**
- The inherited estate will be used in a way consistent with the PPFM.
- The estate can be used to cover the regulatory capital requirements of the with-profits fund.
- The estate can be used to cover new business strain.
- The estate can be used to establish a strong capital position to use in literature;
- to attract new policyholders.
- A large estate allows the insurer to sell business with high acquisition costs;
- or high initial guarantees.
- A strong capital position could be used to improve the company’s credit rating;
- so, the cost of borrowing can be reduced.

**Investment strategy**
- A large estate provides the company more flexibility in its investment strategy;
- allowing the company to invest in riskier assets in search of higher returns.
- For example, investing more in equities.
- The higher returns would lead to higher bonuses and payouts for with-profits policyholders.

**Tax**
- The estate can be used to pay taxes incurred when distributing surplus to shareholders.
- Some have criticised the use of the estate to pay shareholder tax;
- however, it is considered a general practice in some jurisdictions.

**Expenses**
- The estate could be used to cover new business expenses or expense overruns.
- So, if actual expenses turn out higher than might be considered acceptable to charge to policyholders, the company may only charge a portion to the asset share;
- and the remaining to the estate.
- Some modern contracts deduct explicit expense charges from asset shares (in a similar way as for unit-linked contracts).
- For these contracts, the excess of the expenses incurred and shareholder transfers over the contractual charges will be charged to the estate.

**Payouts**
- The estate can be used to fund payouts in excess of the asset share.
- So, an estate can support the company’s smoothing policy.
- The larger the estate, the greater the degree of smoothing that can be applied.
- The estate can also be used to meet the cost of guarantees when they bite, i.e. if there is guaranteed payouts in excess of the asset share.
- In some cases, payouts that are in excess of asset shares are then charged back to with-profits policyholders rather than to the estate, e.g. the cost of benefits on death would be shared amongst all policies within the cohort.

<u><b>Q1(iii): How would the company distribute surplus capital?</b></u>
**Overall**
- The overall distribution could be done implicitly via addition to asset share;
- or explicitly through bonuses, e.g. regular / terminal / special bonus or refund of charges.

**Addition to asset share**
- The surplus could be distributed by enhancing policyholder’s asset share.
- This is the simplest way to distribute surplus.
- As asset share is considered the starting point for fair value this approach can be seen as equitable.
- However, policyholders are unlikely to understand this approach and may fail to fully appreciate that they have benefitted from the surplus.
- Policyholders would receive higher terminal bonus at maturity due to the increased asset share;
- and surrender values would also be higher.
- Similarly, the surplus could be added to the shadow fund for accumulating with-profits policies.
- This could be made by increasing the unit price or the number of units for unitised contracts.
- All asset shares can be increased by a flat percentage.
- However, it would not be as simple as using a percentage increase found by dividing the desired distribution amount by the aggregate asset share;
- as the increase in asset share would alter the cost of guarantees.

**Regular bonus**
- The surplus can be distributed via higher regular bonus throughout the policy lifetime.
- However, once a regular bonus is declared it is guaranteed;
- and so, increases reserves and reduces investment flexibility.
- This method would not lead to increased payout if the asset share is greater than the guaranteed value (as any increase in regular bonus would be offset by reduction in terminal bonus).
- So, an increase in regular bonus needs to be matched by a corresponding increase in asset shares.
- It is possible an increase in asset share could lead to an increase in regular bonus;
- but this would only be likely if the increase in asset shares was significant.
- In conclusion, it is unlikely that regular bonus would be used to distribute the surplus. It is much more likely that asset shares will be enhanced so that either a special bonus or higher terminal bonus can be paid.

**Terminal bonus**
- The surplus could be distributed by paying a large terminal bonus.
- Distributing through terminal bonus rather than regular bonus gives greater flexibility.

**Special bonus**
- The surplus could be distributed through a special bonus.
- This would be added in a similar way to regular bonus and cannot be taken away.
- This is easier to explain to policyholders than an addition to asset share;
- and easier for them to understand;
- and should create good publicity for the insurance company.
- The special bonus should not create an expectation of future such bonus as it is one-off.
- The special bonus would lead to an immediate transfer to shareholders and this cost needs to be allowed for.
- Shareholders may prefer the special bonus approach as it leads to earlier shareholder transfers than a method involving higher terminal bonuses.
- A cash bonus may not be permitted under regulation.
- If addition to asset share is applicable to all policy types, the resulting special bonus may differ by product type;
- depending on the run-off profile;
- and guarantee costs.
- It will still be necessary to enhance asset shares as described above;
- otherwise, the addition of the special bonus would result in reduced terminal bonus.
- This approach would increase the guaranteed benefits within the fund;
- but although this might be preferred by the policyholders;
- it increases the reserves and regulatory capital requirements for the fund;
- and so, offsets to some extent the de-risking benefits which have generated the additional surplus in the first place.

**Refund of charges**
- The company could refund some of the charges that have been deducted from the asset share (e.g. for the cost of guarantees or cost of capital);
- as existing policyholders who have paid these charges have directly contributed to the estate.

**Selection**
- The policyholders should be informed after the date at which the distribution is effective;
- to prevent policyholders from exploiting the situation.
- For example, single premium bond policyholders could temporarily switch out of other unit-linked funds and into the with-profits fund in order to qualify for the bonus (and then switch back to unit-linked funds again).
- The company should also use a distribution method that avoids a mass lapse after declaring.

**Selecting policyholders**
- The Board needs to decide who would be eligible for such a distribution.
- The key factors would be to treat all customers fairly;
- and be equitable between different groups and generations of policyholders.
- The Board could decide to favour one group over another;
- e.g. those who have suffered low bonus rates due to lower returns could be given a larger share of surplus distribution;
- or those who have contributed the most to the de-risking could be given a larger share.
- Alternatively, the surplus occurred at a point in time;
- so, all current policyholders have the right to the distribution.
- The Board may distribute to those who contributed most to the estate.
- The Board may distribute to those who have invested over the longest periods;
- however, this might look arbitrary and unfair as the most of the estate will have been inherited from the previous generation.
- The company needs to consider carefully whether it would be fair to distribute less to policyholders simply because they chose a product with a strong guarantee.
- The company needs to ensure that there is fair treatment between the two main types of with-profits policyholder (conventional and accumulating);
- and may need to consider whether different treatment is appropriate in order to achieve this.

**General considerations**
- The Board should consider any past communications and PRE from the surplus distribution.
- The company should consider the views of the Chief Actuary.
- The company should consider the views of the With-Profits Actuary.
- The Chief Actuary should have a view on the appropriate amount to distribute;
- based on impact to solvency.
- In addition to any With-Profits Actuary, there may be a With-Profits Committee in place;
- which would help inform the Board’s view on fairness.
- The Board should also consider the manner of any previous surplus distributions have been made by the company;
- or by other companies in a similar position.

<u><b>Q1(iv): What are the issues to consider when closing with-profits fund to new business?</b></u>
**Run-off plan**
- The company needs to understand how the business will run-off.
- As new business volumes have been low, the fund may be in run-off already.
- The regulator may require the company to have a run-off plan.
- The company may already have the plan, particularly if it has an effective risk management.
- It may be there is no change needed in the way the fund is managed;
- as issues have already been considered;
- and actions taken (e.g. the de-risking exercise).
- The run-off plan should consider the balance between different product types overtime as this may influence management actions.

**Investment strategy**
- The fund is strong, so it has lots of investment freedom.
- So, a large proportion could be invested in equities and property in search of higher returns.
- However, the strategy might need to change as the business runs off.
- As the business nears maturity, guarantees may become more significant;
- and so, reduces investment freedom;
- e.g. higher proportion invested into fixed-interest bonds.
- Alternatively, derivatives could be used to hedge the guarantees (e.g. put options).
- The impact of different investment approaches on policyholders should be considered.
- For example, a low-risk investment strategy would reduce capital requirements;
- allowing the estate to be distributed faster.
- This benefits policyholders near maturity (who otherwise miss out on distribution of capital tied up to support a riskier investment strategy);
- however, would disadvantage policyholders furthest from maturity;
- as the lower returns would lead to lower bonuses and payouts.

**Expenses**
- The expenses to administer the business will change during the run-off and needs to be managed.
- Per policy costs are likely to increase due to overheads spread across fewer policies;
- particularly if administration is done in house.
- Closure to new business will incur additional costs;
- e.g. redundancy costs for those involved in product development, sales and processing applications.
- However, the company will continue to sell without-profits business, so staff may be redeployed elsewhere in the company and overheads can be spread over all the business.
- The company could consider outsourcing the administration of the with-profits business.
- The Board could limit the impact of increasing per policy costs on asset shares;
- by charging the increase in expenses to the estate.
- It may be considered reasonable for fixed costs directly attributable to with-profits business to be charged to the with-profits fund.
- However, the company should consider what action may be taken to prevent fixed costs becoming an increasing burden on policy asset shares;
- and on future payouts.
- The company may continue to sell the single premium bond as a unit-linked product.
- So, the accumulating with-profits bond policyholders would continue to benefit from being administered on a system with a stable policy count.
- However, the conventional with-profits policies are unlikely to be administered on the same system as other contracts and so will be unable to share their fixed costs with other business.
- It might be worth following a strategy (such as outsourcing) that increases per policy costs now;
- to reduce the impact of escalating per policy costs in the future.
- The inherent cross subsidy between product types would need to be considered.

**Distribution of the estate**
- The fund is strong, so there is a considerable estate to distribute to policyholders.
- The Board needs to decide how to distribute the estate equitably over time.
- The estate is likely to be distributed at regular intervals as the business is run off;
- to avoid disproportionately large payouts to the last few policyholders (the tontine effect).
- If the estate is distributed gradually each year it will benefit policyholders that exit a long time after distributions start relative to those that exit soon after.
- The company would have to decide whether it considers this to be fair.
- Distributions can only be made to the extent that they do not reduce benefit security to an unacceptable level.
- This means some capital needs to be retained in the short term, so that policies exiting early on would not benefit from the estate to its full extent.
- A reattribution of the estate may be able to allow a faster distribution of the estate to policyholders.

**Bonus philosophy**
- Distributing the estate could reduce the company’s ability to smooth bonuses in the future.
- The company may need to reduce regular bonus rates as the business runs off;
- as higher guarantees require more capital to be retained;
- which delays the distribution of the estate and so increases the tontine effect.
- Shifting towards terminal bonus will improve flexibility for the company during the run-off phase.
- But the company will have to consider the expectations of policyholders in doing this;
- and the possible impact on withdrawal rates.
- The company may need to reduce the level of smoothing in order to allow capital to be released.

**Reserves / technical provisions**
- The valuation assumptions may need to change, e.g. to reflect increased per policy expenses.
- Operational risks may increase in the short term.

**Policyholder communication**
- The company needs to communicate the decisions to policyholders.
- The company may set a customer retention team to avoid mass lapse when policyholders hear the news.
- The company needs to communicate with brokers to maintain their confidence to ensure future without-profits sales.
- The company needs to communicate with the market to protect its share price.
- The company needs to decide how to communicate its decision to policyholders;
- e.g. in a more customer-friendly format than communications to brokers or the market.
- The PPFM may cover the actions the company would take if it closed to new business.
- The closure of the fund will require changes to such literature.
- If changes to the principles set out in the PPFM are required;
- then the company should give three months’ notice of the changes.
- Any significant changes to the management of the with-profits business would need to be communicated to policyholders so that they could assess the likely impact on their benefits.
- Closure to new business has, in the past, been associated with weak funds and so closure could alarm policyholders unnecessarily.
- The company would wish to prevent a large number of withdrawals from the fund;
- as this would make the problem of rising per policy costs worse.
- Furthermore, policyholders may be tempted to cancel policies with guarantees erroneously
- Communicating possible future distributions will need to be worded carefully.
- The company would not wish to set unreasonably low expectations.
- So, policyholders leave thinking that it is not worth retaining their policy.
- Also, it would not wish to encourage anti-selective behaviour by policyholders hoping to maximise their value from the distribution of the estate;
- e.g. through policy increments;
- or switching into the accumulating with-profits fund from other unit-linked funds.
- There will be an increased number of queries from worried policyholders to deal with.
- There may be specific regulations in place that have to be followed on formal closure.
- For example, the company might have to provide certain information to the regulator.
- It might also have to write to all policyholders in the fund giving prescribed information.

**Focus on without-profits business**
- It might become harder to sell unit-linked business if there is no longer the option to switch to accumulating with-profits fund.
- The company needs to ensure that it has sufficient capital in the without-profits fund to support its plans for higher levels of without-profits new business.

**Other points**
- The company should make sure that all of its actions are consistent with the principles of treating customers fairly.
- The Chief Actuary and the With-Profits Actuary will have to follow professional guidance.
- Staff morale may fall, particularly in with-profits teams.
- There is a danger that service standards could fall as the portfolio runs down;
- which could lead to higher customer complaints;
- and higher withdrawals.
- The company should consider the impact of closure on its embedded value;
- e.g. the impact of higher per policy expenses, different investment mix and the absence of with-profits new business in the future.
